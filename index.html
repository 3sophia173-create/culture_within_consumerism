Culture within Consumerism

Emma Day | Mariela Gomez | WRIT 20833 | Fall 2025

Research Question

How do variations in culture, age, and clothing type change how women view clothing as an expression, and how does it affect their self-expression?

Background: Changes in culture have extreme effects on how people dress, and these shifts can confine individuals to certain stereotypes. For instance, crop tops are a newer fashion trend that evolved from more modest dressing styles of the early 1900s. This shift has influenced how cultures view modesty and how women are allowed to express themselves.

Ethical Considerations: We scraped data from a public platfrom (YouTube). When refrencing comments, we do not expose usernames to protect personal privacy. We also relied on the assumption that public comments are proper components for research.

Code Example

Here's how we implemented the sentiment analysis using vaderSentiment:

sentiment_analysis.py
from vaderSentiment.vaderSentiment import SentimentIntensityAnalyzer

# Im installing the sentiment analyzer
analyzer = SentimentIntensityAnalyzer()

# Calculate compound sentiment score for each review
df['sentiment_score'] = df['review_clean'].apply(lambda x: analyzer.polarity_scores(x)['compound'])

# Categorize sentiment
df['sentiment_label'] = df['sentiment_score'].apply(
    lambda x: 'positive' if x > 0.05 else ('negative' if x < -0.05 else 'neutral')
)

# Show distribution of sentiment labels

Key Findings

The computational analysis revealed 3 major insights:

  1. Finding 1: Public support for modest dressing is common in sampled YouTube comments.

    The comments emphasize respect and support for a creator choosing modest gym wear.

  2. Finding 2: Practical attributes (fit, size, comfort) dominate consumer talk about clothing.

    Across the Kaggle reviews "fit", "size", and "comfortable" appear as the most frequent and influential terms — they predict positive reviews strongly.

  3. Finding 3: Clothing functions as both self-expression and social signal.

    Topic clusters showed that clothing discussions mix personal feelings (confidence, comfort) with social evaluation (respect, judgment, cultural norms), meaning clothing talk is both individual and collective.

📐 Classification Logic

This project connects to Classification Logic because our datasets show how conversations around culture within consumerism are already formated through classification systems even before we analyzed them. The way we collected, named and structured our information in our datasets heavily influences where the patterns will become visible. An example is that our data highlights certain reccuring themes like people talking about modesty, feeling comfortable with themselves, what the public may think, or even personal morals, not only because they fall into these basic categories but because the dataset is structured to frame them this way. Our findings show that culture within consumerism is discussed heavily through recurring patterns that reflect how society classifies "ethical clothing", "modesty in fashion", or "responsible" choices. Instead of us generally creating categories, the datasets showcase pre-existing cultural classifications that influence people when talking about these topics. This connects direvctly with Classification Logic because it demonstartes how information inputed into datsets relect certain stereotypes or assumptions and cultural structures.

Question: What nuances are lost when we reduce complex cultural expressions to computational categories?

Our findings suggest that when conversations are being had and are conscious about culture within consumerism the framework becomes limited to certain labels like "responsible", "sustainable" or "ethical", essentially getting rid of the deeper nuances. An example is that people may experience identity crisis, guilt over not fitting in, or cultural expectations, yet these ties don't always appear on datasets.

🤖 AI Agency

The use of topic modeling and sentiment analysis demonstrates AI Agency concerns. While the algorithms appear to "discover" meaning, the interpretation and framing of results remains entirely human. The system does no actually understand the content it is analyzing and has no emotional connection. AI uses patter matching and human-defined categories to infer emotions and themes that it produces in the sentiment analysis. This creates concern because the algorithm uses past human decisions to determine "positive" and "negative" commects so really humans shape the entire process. The information it produces originates from human opinion so we have to be careful when fully agreeing with AI because it can show a bias of its creator.
